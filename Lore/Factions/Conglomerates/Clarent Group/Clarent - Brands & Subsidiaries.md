# Clarent Group — Brands & Subsidiaries

*Three layers. Most people only ever see the bottom one.*

---

## The Three-Tier Structure

Clarent Group does not present itself to consumers. It presents its brands. The ownership structure is technically public record — buried in Federal States corporate filings that nobody reads — but at street level it is functionally invisible. A resident of New Halcyon can spend their entire life buying Clarent products without ever encountering the Clarent name.

### Tier 1 — Clarent Group
The parent. Invisible at street level. Appears on corporate documents, internal communications, and the occasional financial report that surfaces in specialist media. Owns everything below it. Makes no consumer-facing products under its own name.

### Tier 2 — Grouped Brands
Mid-layer holding identities. Each grouped brand manages a cluster of individual labels, handles regional distribution, and maintains the fiction of independence for the brands beneath it. Some grouped brands are legacy acquisitions — companies that were once genuine independents and still carry that cultural residue even though they have answered to Clarent for decades.

**Nestlé** — Clarent's most significant grouped brand. Legacy Swiss identity deliberately preserved because it carries premium connotations in certain demographics. Manages Clarent's higher-end synthetic and imported food lines. Most residents who know the Nestlé name do not know it answers to Clarent.

**Fallow & Sons** — American-facing grouped brand. Frontier-coded name chosen to feel generational and domestic. Manages Clarent's mainstream synthetic food labels and commissary supply contracts. The name implies a family farm that has never existed.

**Meridian Foods** — manages Clarent's drink and supplement lines including the premium UT-import beverage range. Cleaner aesthetic than Fallow & Sons, aimed at middle-tier residents who want to feel like they are making a health-conscious choice.

*Additional grouped brands pending district design — each major district in New Halcyon likely has at least one Clarent grouped brand with local cultural coding.*

### Tier 3 — Individual Brands
The street-facing layer. These are the names on packaging, on vendor signage, on commissary menus. Some are legacy brands absorbed through acquisition and still carry their original identity. Some were created by Clarent wholesale. Most residents experience these as independent companies.

## Synthetic vs Premium Lines — The Doritos Model

The clearest illustration of how Clarent's tier system works at the product level is their treatment of surviving pre-Catalyst brands. Using Doritos as an example:

**Doritos** — the base synthetic line. Calorically complete. Micronutrient deficient. Cheap, available everywhere, sold in every commissary in New Halcyon. The packaging uses the legacy brand identity. The product inside is synthetic food with flavouring.

**Doritos Max** — the mid-tier line. Marginally better ingredient sourcing, slightly less synthetic content, more sophisticated flavour engineering. Still deficient. Costs noticeably more. The packaging signals premium without delivering it nutritionally.

**Doritos Reserve** — the premium line, distributed under the Nestlé grouped brand. Contains genuine UT-sourced micronutrient content. Actually nutritionally meaningful. Priced to be a middle-tier occasional purchase or a wealthy resident's staple. The word "Reserve" is deliberate — it echoes [[The Reserve]]'s institutional language of stability and trust, applied to a snack food.

This structure repeats across Clarent's entire product catalogue. Every legacy brand has a synthetic base line and a premium UT-import variant. The gap between them is nutritional, economic, and in the long run biological.

## The Franchise Network

Beyond its own brands, Clarent operates an aggressive franchise system across New Halcyon's vendor economy. Independent food vendors — district market stalls, building commissaries, street carts — are approached by Clarent enforcement representatives and offered franchise agreements.

The offer is structured to be difficult to refuse:
- Clarent supply chain access at rates independent vendors cannot match
- Brand identity from one of the recognised Tier 3 labels
- "Protection" from supply disruptions — which Clarent's own enforcement arm is capable of causing

Vendors who accept become Clarent franchises. They display a Tier 3 brand, buy stock through Clarent's supply chain, and return a percentage of revenue to the grouped brand above them. Most of their customers have no idea.

Vendors who refuse tend to find supply chains unreliable, competitor franchises opening nearby, and occasional visits from Clarent security that don't technically constitute threats.

The result: a significant portion of New Halcyon's apparently independent food retail is Clarent. The exact proportion is unknown and deliberately obscured. Estimates among factions who track this range from 40% to over 60% of district-level food vendors in non-forgotten areas.

## UT Import Distribution

Clarent purchases the majority of its United Territories GMO imports through [[Continental Freight Authority]] logistics channels. This is a source of structural tension — Clarent's premium product lines depend on CFA's import pipeline, which means Clarent's highest-margin business is partially hostage to a relationship with an entity whose loyalty is unknown.

Clarent has spent considerable resources attempting to develop alternative import routes. None have reached the scale of the CFA channel. This dependency is not discussed publicly by Clarent's Senate.
