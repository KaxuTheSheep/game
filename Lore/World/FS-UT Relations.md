# FS–UT Relations

*Two successor states born from the same crisis. Neither can fully separate. Neither can fully trust. Neither can fully win.*

---

## The Historical Root

The Federal States and United Territories are not ancient rivals. They were one country until 2039. The split was not a war — it was a fracture along pre-existing fault lines when different regions responded differently to [[The Catalyst (2039)]].

The UT views itself as the part of the old nation that refused to sell its sovereignty.

The FS views itself as the part that dealt with reality and survived.

Neither uses the original country's name. Neither fully recognises the other's legitimacy. Both consider themselves the true continuation. This creates a bitterness that persists across generations — not the clean hatred of traditional enemies, but the specific resentment of a family that split over money.

---

## The Dependency Problem

On paper the Federal States should be stronger. It has more financial institutions, more corporations, greater industrial capacity, greater population density, and more international economic influence.

The problem is food — specifically micronutrients.

### The Micronutrient Trap

The Federal States is not starving. [[Synthetic Food]] solved calories decades ago. An FS citizen can consume sufficient calories indefinitely. The problem is nutrition.

The United Territories' GMO agricultural system produces specialised foods containing micronutrients and biological compounds that are difficult or uneconomical to manufacture synthetically. As long as UT exports continue, FS citizens remain fed, productive, and functional.

If those exports stop, the consequences are **gradual rather than immediate:**

- Declining population health
- Rising medical costs
- Worsening [[Framework]] complications (many augments interact with biological systems; nutritional deficiency degrades that interaction)
- Reduced workforce quality
- Compounding social instability

It is a slow societal degradation rather than a famine. The absence of a single dramatic crisis is precisely what makes it politically difficult to address — nobody starves on a specific day that can be pointed to.

## Why the FS Doesn't Invade

The Federal States is, in narrow military terms, capable of projecting more force than the United Territories. The question of why it hasn't acted is answered by several compounding reasons.

**No central authority.** The Federal States barely functions as a unified nation. Conglomerates frequently disagree on major policy questions. Many would suffer enormous losses from conflict; others might profit. There is no government capable of declaring war.

**Economic risk.** The corporations that dominate the Federal States profit from stability. War threatens supply chains, markets, infrastructure, and investor confidence. The costs are enormous and fall unevenly — meaning some conglomerates would actively lobby against conflict regardless of strategic arguments for it.

**The occupation problem.** Capturing farmland is easier than operating it. The UT's food system is deeply dependent on its own scientists, agricultural specialists, biotech infrastructure, and state-managed logistics. A military campaign that destroys the UT risks destroying the food system the FS needs. Occupation without cooperation produces ruin, not supply.

**Mutual destruction calculus.** The UT holds a second lever beyond food. It controls the biological components — grown organs, non-framework tissue, biotech replacements — that Framework-augmented FS citizens require if they want to emigrate or undergo biological reconstruction. A FS that went to war with the UT would be closing that door permanently for its own population.

---

## Why the UT Doesn't Cut Off Food

The UT's leverage is real but not unlimited.

The Federal States remains the UT's largest customer and most significant trading partner. A complete food cutoff would trigger UT economic recession, political instability, and likely escalation. The UT wants leverage — it does not want the consequences of using it fully.

The food dependency is a weapon the UT cannot fire without also shooting itself.

## The Cold War Reality

The relationship resembles a cold war more than a traditional rivalry.

Both sides need each other. Both sides resent each other. Both sides believe their system is demonstrably superior. Neither side wants to be the one that pushes too far first.

Proxy conflicts, trade disputes, information warfare, and factional politics fill the space that open conflict cannot.

---

## Ideological Conflict

The disagreement runs deeper than economics.

The UT sees the Federal States as proof that corporate power inevitably consumes democracy. To many UT citizens, New Halcyon is not an impressive experiment. It is a warning.

The Federal States sees the United Territories as a society that sacrificed individual freedom for collective security. FS citizens are educated — through corporate schools, subscription learning platforms, and network-delivered content — with a consistent message:

*"The UT wants to control your life."*

Specific narrative lines repeated across generations:
- "The UT decides what job you'll have."
- "The UT decides what enhancements you're allowed."
- "The UT suppresses innovation."
- "The UT fears freedom."

These are not straightforward lies. They contain elements of truth, framed ideologically. The UT does manage its citizens' lives more directly than the FS. The FS framing simply omits that FS citizens are managed just as thoroughly — by corporations rather than a state.

The UT's counter-narrative is equally selective:
- "FS citizens are customers, not people."
- "The Framework is a leash with a subscription fee."
- "New Halcyon is what happens when nobody is in charge."

Neither side completely understands the other. Most citizens know the other primarily through media and propaganda.

## Defection and Emigration

The Federal States is relatively open informationally. People can access foreign media, communicate internationally, and discover contradictions in the official narrative. Many eventually realise that UT citizens live longer, eat better, and have lower poverty rates.

Some attempt to leave.

### The Medical Reality of Emigration

Leaving is possible but medically costly. SOMA augments physically integrate with the body. Removing them is straightforward. The problem is what remains: biological gaps where augments replaced or supplemented natural function. Filling those gaps requires biological material — donor organs, grown tissue, biotech replacements.

The United Territories produces these components. It controls access to them. Its willingness to provide them to departing FS citizens is limited and conditional.

### The UT's Managed Intake System

The UT does not operate a humanitarian open-door policy. From its perspective, an arriving FS citizen may be:

- Heavily dependent on proprietary or corporate-linked hardware
- Culturally hostile or politically radicalised by decades of FS propaganda
- Potentially carrying corporate surveillance infrastructure in their own body
- Potentially a valuable skilled worker
- Potentially a liability

The UT treats emigration pragmatically. Intake is managed, selective, and assessed on individual usefulness and risk. Those with skills the UT values — scientists, engineers, specialists — receive more favourable consideration. Those without demonstrable value face a longer, harder process.

This is not stated publicly as official policy. It operates as a de facto reality.

### Class and the Emigration Trap

Wealthy FS citizens can stockpile options — maintaining some non-augmented biological capacity, accessing black market biological components, or cultivating relationships with UT-adjacent brokers. For them, emigration is expensive and difficult but achievable.

For poor citizens, fully augmented and without resources, emigration is effectively impossible. The trap is not legal — nobody is forbidden from leaving. The trap is biological and economic.

## Country D's Role

[[Country D]] — the regulated democratic bloc — maintains SOMA infrastructure capable of supporting Framework-augmented individuals. This allows FS dissidents and refugees to arrive in Country D without immediately losing augment function.

Country D cannot perform full biological reconstruction itself — it lacks the UT's biotech capacity. But it can maintain FS citizens in their current augmented state while longer-term arrangements are made. This makes it a viable first destination for those who cannot secure UT intake approval directly.

Some conglomerates quietly fund Country D emigration pathways as a social stability measure — removing radicalised or troublesome individuals from New Halcyon is cheaper than managing them.

---

## The Real Fear on Both Sides

Beneath the propaganda, the trade disputes, the proxy conflicts, and the migration politics, both sides share a version of the same fear.

The Federal States fears discovering it genuinely cannot function without the United Territories — that the dependency is not manageable but existential.

The United Territories fears discovering it cannot safely coexist beside the Federal States indefinitely — that the pressure will eventually produce a confrontation it cannot survive militarily.

Everything else grows from that tension.
