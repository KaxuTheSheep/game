# The Catalyst (2039)

The single most significant event in the world's history. Not a war, not a revolution — a bankruptcy. The moment the state stopped being the state.

---

## What Happened

A cascading AI-driven financial market crash, compounded by years of eroding tax base and unsustainable sovereign debt, pushed the Federal States' federal government into formal default. Unable to secure international bailout terms (Country B refused on strategic grounds; Country D offered terms the government rejected as politically untenable), the federal government accepted a domestic bailout — structured by its own largest corporate creditors.

The terms required the sell-off of core state functions: currency minting, infrastructure management, justice systems, and eventually policing. The state did not collapse overnight — it signed itself away, incrementally, in exchange for short-term solvency.

---

## The Split

The sell-off terms were not accepted uniformly. Western and midland states — broadly more self-sufficient, with stronger agricultural and renewable energy bases — refused to ratify the federal bailout agreement and broke away. This fracture eventually formalised into [[The United Territories]].

The remaining eastern coastal corridor, dependent on financial markets and corporate infrastructure, had less leverage to refuse. It became [[The Federal States]] as it exists in the present day.

Neither nation claims to have caused the split. Both claim to be the legitimate continuation of the old country. Neither uses the original name.

---

## What It Produced

- [[The Reserve]] — the former central bank, privatised wholesale as a minting company
- The conditions for [[New Halcyon]] and Cities 1 through X — early privatised city experiments, most of which failed
- The legal and cultural vacuum that New Halcyon's entire present-day structure grew into
- The food dependency dynamic — the Federal States lost its agricultural heartland to the United Territories in the split, a consequence nobody fully planned for at the time

---

## Tone Note

The Catalyst should never read as a dramatic villain's move or a coup. It was mundane. Committees voted. Lawyers drafted documents. The state ended not with a bang but with a restructuring agreement. That mundanity is the point — and is more unsettling than any single dramatic act.
